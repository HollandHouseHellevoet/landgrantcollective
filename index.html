How We See OSU's Position

A mission that extends well beyond Stillwater.

Oklahoma State University is the largest higher education system in the state. Its health system carries a mission that extends well beyond Stillwater into rural communities where care is becoming harder to find and harder to sustain. Into rural hospitals that are financially fragile. Into independent physician practices across the state that are being slowly consolidated out of existence.

OSU also carries something else: an AA- bond rating from both S&P and Fitch, built through decades of financial discipline. That rating is an asset. It represents optionality: the ability to act when others cannot.

The question we came to discuss is what kind of action protects that optionality, and what kind expands it.

The Context That Shapes Everything

The enrollment cliff is not a projection. It is demographic math.

Birth rates fell in 2007. Those children turn 18 in 2025. The decline in college-age population runs through 2039. Every university in the country is preparing for it. OSU has been preparing since 2018.

The institutions that navigate this window well will be the ones that built revenue architecture independent of enrollment before the pressure arrived. The ones that did not will be managing fixed obligations against a shrinking revenue base.

This is the context inside which every major capital decision OSU makes over the next five years should be evaluated.

The Capital OSU Already Has

These are not costs. They are capital flows.

OSU Health writes checks every month. Health benefits for employees. Workers' compensation. Medical malpractice. Directors and officers liability. Errors and omissions. Property and casualty. These are treated as costs. They are not costs. They are capital flows, and they are large.

Here is where those flows go today. Roughly 83 cents of every premium dollar pays claims. The remaining 17 cents funds something else entirely. A portion covers the carrier's administration. Another portion pays broker commissions: commissions the carrier pays, not OSU, for placing OSU's business with that carrier. What remains, 3 to 7 cents on every dollar, becomes surplus. The carrier's retained capital. UnitedHealth Group holds $25.5 billion of it. Elevance holds $40.3 billion. OSU's premium dollars helped build those numbers. OSU owns none of it.

Then there is float. The premium arrives at the carrier on the first of the month. Claims are paid over the following 30, 60, 90 days. During that gap, the carrier holds OSU's money and invests it. The returns belong to the carrier. Warren Buffett built Berkshire Hathaway on this mechanic, not by picking stocks, but by owning insurance companies that generated float. He has been explicit about this for sixty years. The carriers processing OSU's claims run the identical model.

This is not a complaint about carriers. It is a description of an architecture. The architecture has a geometry. Capital flows in one direction. The question is whether it has to.

A Different Geometry

The Land Grant Collective.

MedMerge builds the infrastructure that sits between the employer and the claim, across every line of insurance, every administrative function, every vendor relationship. For OSU Health, that infrastructure has a name: The Land Grant Collective.

The Land Grant Collective deploys the MedMerge model with OSU Health as the anchor: the institution at the center of a shared infrastructure that every rural hospital and independent practice in the state can plug into. The anchor does not become an insurance company. It does not administer claims or set reserves or manage stop-loss counterparty relationships. What it does is own. Ownership and operations are separable. In insurance they have been artificially conflated for forty years, to the benefit of the entities that prefer employers remain on the paying end of the line.

Inside the Land Grant Collective, the float stays inside the structure. The surplus accumulates as equity on a balance sheet the anchor holds. The underwriting margin stops leaving. Costs drop 20 to 30 percent from the first month of participation, not as a projection, but as a function of moving from retail fully-insured pricing to wholesale self-funded rates.

What This Means for Rural Oklahoma

OSU becomes the infrastructure they cannot operate without.

OSU Health has relationships with rural hospitals across the state. Those hospitals are financially fragile. They lack the actuarial scale to self-fund. They lack the purchasing density to negotiate meaningfully with carriers, pharmacy benefit managers, or stop-loss providers. They are paying retail for everything.

Inside the collective, that changes immediately. Each rural hospital that joins moves from retail to wholesale pricing across every insurance line. The 20 to 30 percent cost reduction that applies to OSU applies to them as well.

OSU does not acquire these hospitals. OSU becomes the infrastructure they cannot operate without. The hospitals stay independent. Their balance sheets stay their own. What changes is the economics, and the relationship.

The Fiserv Position

Fiserv does not own a single bank. It owns what the banks cannot operate without: the core infrastructure that makes a bank function. Each bank is independent. But migration away means rebuilding from scratch. The banks stay. The economics flow to Fiserv. OSU as anchor is the Fiserv position. Applied to rural Oklahoma healthcare.

The Questions OSU Is Already Asking

Four answers.

Yes. Does this help place residents across the state?

A network of rural hospitals inside the collective gives OSU Health a statewide footprint of institutions with formal economic relationships with OSU, not vendor relationships, but infrastructure relationships. Placing residents inside that network is a natural extension of what already exists. The hospitals want the residents. The residents get rural experience. OSU gets statewide placement without acquiring the facilities.

Yes. Does this help build a physician network throughout Oklahoma?

Every independent practice that joins the collective gains the same economics the hospitals do: wholesale pricing, shared infrastructure, reduced administrative burden. For an independent physician practice in rural Oklahoma, the cost reduction is not marginal. It is the difference between viability and consolidation.

OSU Health, as the anchor of that collective, becomes the institution those practices are economically connected to. Not through employment. Not through acquisition. Through infrastructure. The network builds itself as each practice joins, because each practice has a reason to join that has nothing to do with loyalty and everything to do with economics.

Yes. Does this support Medicaid, Medicare, and a commercial network simultaneously?

Inside the collective, every hospital and independent practice contracts as one entity. That changes everything about payer contracting.

A solo physician in Guymon negotiating alone is invisible to a payer. Inside the Land Grant Collective with OSU Health as the anchor, that same physician is part of a network with scale, actuarial credibility, and a counterparty worth negotiating with. Every rate on every rail improves.

Medicare and Medicaid reimburse based on provider designation and volume. As one entity, the collective qualifies for rates that a solo practice never could. Commercial payers negotiate based on network breadth and patient volume. As one entity, the collective has both. And direct contracts with self-funded Oklahoma employers bypass the carrier entirely — the highest-margin revenue for the physician, the lowest cost for the employer.

Same physician. Same patients. Same care. Three better contracts — because they are no longer negotiating alone.

Yes. Does this create goodwill with independent practices across the state?

This may be the most durable outcome. The independent physician in Enid or Woodward or Durant who joins the collective and sees their administrative costs drop, their insurance spend rationalized, and their economic relationship with OSU Health strengthened does not forget where that came from. OSU becomes the institution that made independent practice more viable, not less. In a state where hospital consolidation has been systematically eliminating independent practice for twenty years, that position is not easily replicated.

What OSU Builds Next

Every capital decision OSU makes over the next five years will build something.

The enrollment cliff does not pause while the decision is made. The rural hospitals do not stabilize while the structure is considered. The independent physicians across the state, in Enid, in Woodward, in Durant, in every county where consolidated systems have not yet arrived, are making their own decisions about how long independent practice remains viable.

OSU has the scale, the mission, and the financial position to become the institution that changes that calculus. Not by acquiring the problem. By owning the infrastructure that makes the problem solvable.

The capital to do it is already leaving OSU's accounts every month. The only question is which balance sheet it builds.
